Reflection on "In Plato's Cave"

As a photography enthusiast myself, Susan Sontag’s essay “In Plato’s Cave” was both insightful and challenging to read. In it, Sontag likens photographs to Plato’s allegory of the cave, arguing that photographs represent a false, distorted reality much like the shadows seen by the prisoners trapped inside the cave. However, while many of Sontag’s points have merit and are well argued, I cannot ultimately agree with with her perspective on nature of photography and its role as a representation of truth. Sontag’s argument mainly comes from the point of view that photography is an act of documentation and journalism, and she mostly ignores the merits of photography as an art form which can create new meaning. To me, photographing something is not simply an act of recording what is already there; it is an act of expression and interpretation that has merit in its own right.

Sontag raises many points that I broadly agree with. She observes that people trust photographs as a record of the truth, saying that we believe, “A photograph passes for incontrovertible proof that a given thing happened.” There are indeed many ways people use photographs as proof that something occurred. People take photographs of their vacations, weddings, and other events to show that they were there. Photographs are also used as identification by governments, as evidence by police, and so on. This assessment is fairly self-evident, as I am sure that almost everyone views having photographic evidence of something makes it seem more trustworthy.

However, Sontag argues that a photograph cannot accurately portray the truth and while she is right in saying so, I disagree with how she ultimately depicts this as a failing of photography. She argues, “Although there is a sense in which the camera does indeed capture reality, not just interpret it, photographs are as much an interpretation of the world as paintings and drawings are.” Certainly, the photographer’s choice of lenses, focal length, f-stop, shutter speed, and even the camera itself will affect the photograph being taken – the camera less so “captures” what is in front of it than it does interpret it. And, of course, there is also interpretation in choosing what to photograph and what is left out. I understand this point implicitly. A photography teacher once taught me to be mindful of what isn’t being photographed. She even said that if I see a group of people all trying to take a picture of something, maybe I should point my camera at them rather than their subject.

Sontag sees the camera’s inability to capture the whole truth as its downfall; the camera can only create false images like the ones in Plato’s cave. If I saw photography as merely a journalistic practice, I might be inclined to agree. But photography is also an art form, even as it seeks to document the world. For example, Bill Cunningham’s iconic street photography is much more than a documentation of the fashion trends of the times. His subjects are often expressive, his shot composition has its own style, and his choice of candid photography has meaning all on its own. Cunningham is quoted as saying, "The best fashion show is definitely on the street. Always has been and always will be." That is not a journalistic observation of the truth, but an artistic statement. One which he conveyed through his own unique style. Photography interprets the real world and creates new meaning from it. Sontag is right when she says this is not “truth.” However, I choose see this as one of its strengths.

Reflection on "AI Slop" Column

Nesrine Malik’s opinion piece titled “With ‘AI slop’ distorting our reality, the world is sleepwalking into disaster” is both a critique and warning about the direction AI is driving our world towards. In it, she argues that the proliferation of AI generated content fuels the right-wing propaganda machine and ultimately overwhelms our already limited attention economy. I am particularly struck by her pointing out that “AI is already being described as the new aesthetic of fascism.” As an artist and as someone who has followed tech and its concerning embrace of fascism, I’ve seen many of Malik’s examples for myself.

I’ve always been interested in how the arts and technology can intersect but, being an artist, I’ve come to the conclusion that I should completely reject AI generated art. I grew up in San Francisco, where I saw artistic communities struggling to survive amidst the tech boom that started in the 90s. I’ve known many artists who had to move out of the city due to rising rents and gentrification. The result was that a city once known for its vibrant arts scene and liberal politics was transformed into a playground for billionaires and fascists like Mark Zuckerberg and Peter Thiel. Therefore, I can’t see AI “art” as anything other than the tech industry’s latest attack on artists. As Malik describes, “To social media giants, content is content; the cheaper it is, the less human labour it involves, the better.” I’ve seen the history of the tech industry driving out artists firsthand and not only is generative AI is the newest development in that history, it also contributes to the United States’ continued slide toward right-wing authoritarianism.

Malik gives many direct examples of AI generated right-wing propaganda. She highlights YouTube videos with made up scenarios where Trump prevails over liberals, the White House creating Studio Ghibli-style images of people being detained by ICE, and the trend of “trad wife” content where men create images of women as submissive homemakers. All of these examples, of course, are incredibly vile. As Malik says, “White supremacy, autocracy, and fetishisation of natural hierarchies in race and gender are packaged as nostalgia for an imagined past.” Though she moves on in her article to talk about content-farming and the attention economy, I can’t stop thinking about the parallels she draws between generative AI and fascism.

The way that the right has wholeheartedly embraced AI is quite fitting, because it is the perfect aesthetic and medium for them to express their ideology. Generative AI can only create content that repackages past works; it cannot create anything totally new. While most people would consider this a major flaw, fascists see this as a strength. In Umberto Eco’s essay “Ur-Fascism,” he outlines fourteen key elements of fascism – two of which are “the cult of tradition” and “the rejection of modernism.” Fascists believe in strictly following an idealized form of tradition. As Eco describes, to them, “When all truth has already been revealed by tradition, no new learning can occur, only further interpretation and refinement.” Since all generative AI can do is interpret and refine past work, its function lines up perfectly with fascist ideology. The ability to create anything new is unwanted and undesirable.

I have to wholeheartedly agree with Malik’s article. My life experience growing up as an artist during the rise of Silicon Valley has shown me firsthand how the tech industry has set the country on a trajectory toward fascism. Additionally, her arguments on how AI is the aesthetic of fascism were eye opening as these two concepts seem linked on a fundamental level. As an artist, I’ve had many reasons to wary of generative AI. Aesthetically, AI generated “art” has no value to me since all it can do is repackage the past. Ideologically, that same functionality means that AI represents a political movement that seeks to destroy everything I value.

Reflection on "Nuclear War" Opinion Piece

W.J. Hennigan’s interactive opinion piece titled “Nuclear War The Rising Risk, and How We Stop It” is an effective and chilling look at the risk of nuclear conflict. While primarily an op-ed article, it makes use of several interactive web design elements to grab the reader’s attention and highlight the horrors of of a potential nuclear conflict. The piece starts with a message that reads, “The risk of nuclear conflict is rising,” set against a flashing red background meant to evoke the image of an explosion. This design hooked me in right away, reminding me of the ever present and ever growing danger of nuclear conflict in today’s world. As the article goes on, it employs several other creative means of conveying its message.

The highlights of the article, for me, are the several animated graphic presentations that unfold as the reader scrolls down. The first asks the reader to imagine if a nuclear weapon is launched. A nuclear missile flies away from our view then detonates, with the explosion expanding as text describes in exhaustive detail what is taking place. After a white flash, the view zooms through fiery scenes of the immediate destruction brought by the detonation, culminating in a depiction of a mushroom cloud. It is very chilling imagery something that has happened before and could very much happen again. Although an animation like this could have been presented as a video, tying it into the user’s inputs as they scroll the article makes it more immersive. As someone who follows international news and geopolitics, I knew on paper what the effects of a nuclear detonation could be. However, seeing the destruction presented in this manner gave me a deeper sense of what could happen.

The graphic showing the number of nuclear arms in the world is equally effective. It starts with a map of the world in 1945, progressing through the years while the user scrolls. Countries with nuclear weapons are highlighted with red dots representing the amount they possess. Over time, the two nuclear weapons possessed by the United States in 1945 expands to over 60,000 worldwide in 1986. While the overall number has decreased since then, we see that other states such as India, Pakistan, and North Korea have developed their own weapons. This is a very creative use of web design in conjunction with data visualization, a chilling display of nuclear proliferation in the world and reminder of the threat these weapons still pose today. It is one thing to see a chart showing the number of weapons, but quite another to witness the spread of nuclear arms throughout time and space.

Sprinkled throughout the article are auto-playing videos of leaders such as Russian President Vladimir Putin and US Defense Secretary Lloyd Austin speaking on the threat of nuclear war. Putin’s remarks are particularly chilling, especially his announcement of withdrawing from the New START treaty to rapturous applause. The fact that the videos play automatically calls attention to them. I’ve read many articles that feature embedded videos which I’ve ignored. The videos in this article, however, call attention to themselves as if to signify that they cannot and should not be ignored. A similar method is employed by the brief overview of India, Pakistan, North Korea, and China’s growing nuclear arsenals. Brief videos of each country parading their nuclear missiles are accompanied by text explaining the country’s growing arsenal and threats to use it. Again, I’ve seen these types of graphics in other articles, but they are usually positioned to the side and easily ignored. The way the article embeds the videos and text as the user scrolls makes it impossible to ignore.

Nuclear war is a terrifying prospect to most people, but few understand how many nuclear weapons are in the world and what things would look like if they are utilized. This opinion piece uses a variety of creative web design, animation, and data visualization techniques to clearly highlight the risk of nuclear conflict. It gives a good sense of the growing threat of a nuclear scenario to someone who may not be well versed in following international news. It also shows, using terrifying imagery, what that kind of destruction would look like. By making use of creative design, the article presents such facts as impossible to ignore.
